I appreciate writing's directness, its ability to relay things as grammatical thought-units, yet I so often hold back from their simplicity in affective terms. It feels a tad vulnerable. Corny, even.Something about digital communication amplifies this hesitation. On social media, I often find myself trapped by perceived tones - sarcasm, anger, kindness - where my writing betrays my intentions. Slouching away from these miscommunications, I've historically invested the bulk of my creative energy towards image-based communication: devising symbols, landscapes, atmospheres, and avatars as a way to ask questions about the world and situate myself within it more broadly.Recently, in this visual mode, I've encountered what feels like a formal wall. As my ambitions shift toward deeper connections with broader audiences, I have a gut-sense the work might require more intimate engagement with narrative language. Having grown accustomed to cloaking my stories in abstraction and symbolism, my attempts at written expression so frequently draw me back to the ineffable… to something weirdly abstract. Sitting down each morning at my keyboard, I languish in digital faktura instead of the referents held within words, patterning pixels that speak to the concrete world before attempting to describe my internal landscape. In my practice, I often feel myself swirling around myself instead of a larger world populated by actual others and can't quite grasp a tether out of that eddy towards more expansive modes of worldbuilding.A breakthrough came in spring 2024 when I began writing essays about the conceptual foundations of my art practice. This type of writing gave me newly structured ways to explore ideas that were difficult to express directly, allowing me to develop and share my thinking process while my visual art practice evolved parallel. Through this work, I discovered scholars whose practices were both inspirationally weird and cogent - particularly Federico Campagna, whose concept of the Abstract General Entity (A.G.E.) resonated with ideas I had been wrestling with for years about critical worldbuilding, abstraction, and their relationship to video game discourse. Still from Black Square, 2020THE TIDE OF CHAOSGrappling with Campagna's work invokes images of chaos: the avalanche, the ocean, the void.
The holistic idea of "the world" is not a given, but rather an order (a cosmos, as the Greeks would say) imposed upon the deluge of raw existence that envelops us at every moment. Looking around a room, one doesn't immediately perceive neatly arranged separate objects. Instead, consciousness first encounters chaos, an undivided torrent of raw perceptions. Upon this tide, the mind imposes a grid that transforms chaos into order, the cosmos in which existence becomes possible. In this sense, everyone exists somewhat outside of the world, surviving on self-made life rafts called "reality."Campagna identifies two primary reality-systems that emerge from this compulsion towards order: Technic and Magic. Technic, our current dominant reality-system, is a world where everything must be quantifiable, productive, and reducible to clear categories - a world of "standing-reserve," to use Heidegger's term, where everything exists primarily as a resource to be utilized.Magic, by contrast, begins with what Technic eliminates: the ineffable. This isn't the magic of Harry Potter where mysterious forces are actually another form of technology waiting to be mastered through incantations (that world still operates firmly within Technic's reality-system, reducing magic to a resource to be put to productive use). Campagna's Magic is something else: a reality-system that recognizes the irreducible mystery at the heart of existence. It doesn't capture or measure this mystery but rather allows it to remain "infinitely active and unapproachable." This doesn't mean Magic rejects order entirely - instead, it creates frameworks that can accommodate both the measurable and the immeasurable, both the explicit and the indescribable. Where Technic seeks to resolve all paradoxes into productive equivalences, Magic allows them to remain as tensions that generate meaning.I’ve found the key to understanding both reality-systems lies in recognizing their constructed nature. By observing how reality is shaped by specific modes of understanding, alternatives become imaginable. This doesn't mean these reality-systems are arbitrary. They emerge from and create real worlds with real consequences. But it does mean they can be modified, questioned, and potentially transformed.Still from Black Square, 2020THE POSSIBILITY OF EXITCampagna suggests that one can not only enter and change worlds but also exit them while still surviving. This isn't merely a theoretical proposition. It's a practical possibility that emerges from understanding the constructed nature of these systems. If worlds are frameworks imposed on chaos to make it habitable, then finding ways to step outside these frameworks becomes possible, even if only temporarily.What does it mean to exit this world and experience a different reality? In "Technic and Magic," Campagna describes how things that slip outside our ordered cosmos become unobservable. They don't disappear ontologically, but they leave the cosmos. They exist in a realm that cannot be captured by descriptive language or reduced to measurable units. This is a space I often attempt to retreat to in my studio. It is a place to fabricate fields of abstraction where meaning refuses to be pinned down, where forms gesture simultaneously towards a concrete world and to affective interiors.The relationship between these realms of the measurable and the ethereal can be illuminated through Goethe's distinction between allegory and symbol:“Allegory transforms the phenomenon into a concept, the concept into an image, but in such a way that the concept always remains bounded in the image, and is entirely to be kept and held in it, and to be expressed by it. Symbolism however transforms the phenomenon into idea, the idea into an image, and in such a way that the idea remains always infinitely active and unapproachable in the image, and even if expressed in all languages, still would remain inexpressible.”This distinction brings into focus what Campagna means by exit not as an abandonment of order altogether, but a shift in how to relate to meaning itself. When cultural expression is treated allegorically, it remain trapped in Technic's reality-system where everything reduces to fixed meanings and productive uses. But symbolic expression offers another possibility. In my practice, this has meant mobilizing abstraction not as a retreat from meaning but rather an engagement with its inexhaustible nature. Abstract symbols become portals toward a reality-system that can accommodate both the measurably comprehensible and the mysteriously infinite.Still from Black Square, 2020VIDEO GAMES AND SIMULATIONOne of Campagna's most prescient illustrations for symbolic coexistence is the medieval garden: "The arrangement of artefacts and a delicate architectural balance - holding together what is 'artificial' and what is 'natural', without sacrificing either force - allowed for the sacred to shine through a space that would otherwise be merely profane. A garden revealed the sacredness that always lies dormant at the heart of every material compound - but which requires a specific symbolic form to be perceptible to human eyes and heart.... A garden of the age typically contained a part that was farmed for fruit and vegetables, a part that was shaped by the geometry of rational architecture and a final part, the bosco, which was left in a state of wilderness, dotted by statues of pagan gods. We can interpret the first of these three parts as the allegory of applied reason, the second as that of pure reason, and the latter, the bosco, as the symbol of the ineffable in its earliest stages of manifestation. There, it was precisely the combination of ineffable wilderness and perfectly crafted artistic objects that allowed the integral universe to emerge."This medieval synthesis of the cultivated and the untamed finds canny parallels in contemporary digital spaces. Where medieval gardens balanced geometric precision with wild growth, digital games create worlds that similarly negotiate between strict computational logic and emergent possibility.  Designer Chaim Gingold observes: "Gardens are dynamic living systems, full of secrets, autonomous agents, transformation, and emergent behaviors. A garden has an inner life all its own. It is a world which goes on without you. Pre-digital games require human agency to animate them, but digital games are animated with the breath of computation, so garden is a tidy metaphor for self-animating systems. Not only are they dynamic, but gardens are reactive to human touch in a variety of ways, just like computers." Campagna extends this metaphor, positioning digital games as unique philosophical instruments for understanding reality-settings:"A video game-world is a 'philosophical tool' in that it makes immediately apparent the connection between its experienceable landscape, and the conceptual structures that sustain and give shape to it. It is both an intelligible realm, susceptible to philosophical analysis, and a space whose very foundations can be reinvented through means of philosophical imagination. While non-ludic digital spaces (like those offered by work programs such as Microsoft Excel) tend to hide their status as 'worlds', video games make no secret of their being cosmoi, that is, worlds endowed with a 'beautiful order' that, I propose, can be philosophically analysed and reinvented."Still from Black Square, 2020APOCALYPSE AS REVELATIONIn my reading of Campagna, the explicit rendering of reality-settings in video games mirrors what he identifies in the actual world as 'apocalypse.' By this, he points not toward catastrophic closure, but toward revelation - the moment when truth about a world becomes visible. And what is revealed? Simply that worlds are dreams of our own making. This doesn't make them any less real; they are indeed the only reality I know how to inhabit. But they are never inert.I've experienced several apocalypses in my life from the passage from childhood to adulthood to the dissolution of previously shared cultural worldviews like the fallout around Gamergate. I can feel such an apocalypse lurking now, weeks away from a US presidential election that seems to promise either dystopic rupture or cataclysmic entrenchment of my current reality-setting. Like a video game making its parameters explicit, these moments of apocalypse reveal the architecture of our worlds even as they transform them beyond our control. In these moments of revelation, I glimpse both the constructed nature of my current reality-setting and the possibility of new configurations.The apocalypse, then, is a useful opening. It is a rupture that renders visible the delicate architecture of our reality-settings and, with practiced attention, cultivates wilder imaginations towards new possibilities. Through this lens, every cultural act becomes a kind of worldbuilding, every essay an experiment in reality-settings, every game a philosophical tool for understanding how worlds are constructed and inhabited. As these moments of unveiling multiply in my life, I'm finding my way through them differently now - not as catastrophes but as passages through the oceanic mess of possibility.Still from Black Square, 2020REFERENCECampagna, Federico.
